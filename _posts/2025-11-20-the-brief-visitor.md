Our story began when we decided we were going to travel to Italy to see our favorite band perform at the Amphitheatre of Pompeii. It sounded like it would be an experience of a lifetime and we couldn't be more excited. Prior to our scheduled departure, we had just completed a program together for seventy five days and were in peak physical and mental condition. No alcohol, no cheat meals, drinking a gallon of water a day, exercising twice a day, and reading ten pages a day. Just before leaving for our trip, we decided to enjoy life, unbeknown to us at the time, that's when our baby was conceived. We traveled to Rome, Dolomites, Naples, Praiano, and Pompeii. The trip of a lifetime—indulging in the finest this earth has to offer. It felt like a dream. All the while our little one was taking shape, quietly coming along with us for the ride.

Once we returned from our trip, it was only a matter of weeks before my partner said the magic words: "guess what?" She didn't have to say anymore, as I knew what was coming next. We fantasized where in Italy we might have conceived the baby. Not until we got the first ultrasound did the nurse tell us we were 11 weeks in. Of course, we kept the excitement to ourselves until about week 13 before we let our families know. Like any parent, we started imagining the life we were about to welcome, shopping at all the baby stores, ordering our first round of diapers and baby bottles, comparing strollers, cribs and bassinets. I'm surprised at the small amount of clothes we did end up buying, but it was too tempting not to pick something up off the shelves in preparation.

Another trip to the family doctor, and we got to hear the heart beat for the first time. A healthy one at that. My partner then got a blood test, and we anxiously awaited the results. Then, our world turned upside down. The news got progressively worse, and we received a diagnosis we had no prior knowledge of. One no parent ever wants to hear. We clung on to what little hope we had, yet it wasn't until the second ultrasound at 16 weeks, where we were given the fatal diagnosis. Acrania—a condition that is incompatible with life.

Disorganized.
No one who has ever watched their ultrasound technician go stone silent, then rush out to fetch the doctor, feels “lucky.”
